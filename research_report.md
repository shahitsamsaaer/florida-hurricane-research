## Hurricane Background (Wikipedia summary)
An Atlantic hurricane or tropical storm is a tropical cyclone that forms in the Atlantic Ocean, usually between June 1 and November 30. These storms have sustained winds of at least 39 mph (34 knots, 63 km/h), and those with winds of at least 74 mph (64 knots, 119 km/h) are classified as hurricanes. The Atlantic hurricane season is the period from June 1 through November 30, though most hurricanes form during August, September, and October. The season's official start was set in the 1930s to coincide with the Great Miami Hurricane of 1926, which devastated the region in September.

Atlantic hurricanes originate from tropical waves off the west coast of Africa. They can cause significant damage through high winds, storm surge, heavy rainfall, and tornadoes. Notable examples include Hurricane Katrina (2005), which caused over $125 billion in damage, and the 1935 Labor Day Hurricane. The National Hurricane Center (NHC) monitors these storms and issues watches and warnings.

Historical data shows an average of 12 named storms, 6 hurricanes, and 2 major hurricanes per season (1981-2010 averages). Climate change may influence future activity.
